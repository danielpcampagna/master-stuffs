### Classification: Scientific Publication

#### Comprehensive Summary

**Title:** Using ontologies to model data protection requirements in workflows

**Authors:** Bartolini, Cesare and Muthuri, Robert and Santos, Cristiana

**Year:** 2015

**Place:** JSAI

**Problem Addressed:**
The publication addresses the challenge of ensuring data protection compliance within workflows. As workflows often involve the processing of personal data, it is crucial to model and enforce data protection requirements effectively to comply with legal regulations such as the GDPR.

**Approach Utilized:**
The authors propose the use of ontologies to model data protection requirements in workflows. Ontologies are a powerful tool for representing knowledge and can be used to ensure that workflows adhere to data protection regulations. The technologies utilized in this approach include:

- **Ontology:** To represent data protection requirements and their relationships.
- **UML (Unified Modeling Language):** For modeling workflows.
- **RDF (Resource Description Framework):** For representing the ontology in a machine-readable format.

**Model/Ontology Introduced:**
The publication introduces an ontology for data protection requirements. The main components of the ontology include:

- **Data Subject:** The individual whose personal data is being processed.
- **Data Controller:** The entity that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data.
- **Data Processor:** The entity that processes data on behalf of the data controller.
- **Processing Activity:** The operations performed on personal data.
- **Legal Basis:** The justification for processing personal data (e.g., consent, contract).

**Methodology Used:**
The authors employed a combination of ontology engineering and workflow modeling techniques. They developed the ontology based on legal texts and expert knowledge in data protection. The workflow models were created using UML and annotated with data protection requirements using the ontology.

**Key Contributions:**
- Development of an ontology to model data protection requirements.
- Integration of the ontology with workflow models to ensure compliance.
- Demonstration of the approach using a case study.

**Advancement of the State-of-the-Art:**
The current model advances the state-of-the-art by providing a systematic way to integrate data protection requirements into workflow models. This ensures that workflows are designed with compliance in mind from the outset, reducing the risk of legal violations.

**Evaluation of the Contribution:**
The authors evaluated their approach using a case study. They modeled a real-world workflow and demonstrated how the ontology could be used to annotate the workflow with data protection requirements. The evaluation showed that the approach could effectively identify and address potential compliance issues.

**Discussion of Results:**
The results of the case study indicated that the ontology-based approach could successfully model data protection requirements in workflows. The authors discussed the benefits of using ontologies, such as improved clarity and consistency in representing requirements. They also highlighted the potential for automation in compliance checking.

**Future Work and Open Issues:**
The authors identified several areas for future work, including:
- Extending the ontology to cover more comprehensive data protection regulations.
- Developing automated tools for compliance checking.
- Evaluating the approach in different domains and with more complex workflows.

Open issues include the need for continuous updates to the ontology to reflect changes in legal regulations and the challenge of integrating the approach with existing workflow management systems.
